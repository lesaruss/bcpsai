AI in Broward classrooms

What AI is, and what it is not

What AI is

  • Supports teaching
  • Supports learning
  • Supports accessibility
  • Supports personalization
  • Supports efficiency

What AI is not

  • Replacing teachers
  • Making educational decisions
  • Grading students independently
  • Replacing human relationships
  • Replacing curriculum

The principle behind every decision

The U.S. Department of Education describes responsible AI in schools like an electric bike, not a robot vacuum: the human stays fully aware and fully in control, while their effort is multiplied. Every AI tool in Broward is meant to be inspectable, explainable, and able to be overridden by an educator.

Humans in the loop, always.

Our approach

The Artificial Intelligence Leadership Framework

Broward's own AILF replaces the generic industry principles previously shown here. It is a district-wide guide for leaders to use AI responsibly, ethically, and strategically, grounded in the Florida Educational Leadership Standards (FELS 1 through 8) and aligned with the district's Strategic Plan.

"Leading ethical, strategic AI integration to advance student success and excellence." The AILF is not a mandate to use AI tools, nor does it replace district policy, professional judgment, or evaluation frameworks. It outlines effective leadership practices for using AI purposefully to support teaching, learning, and organizational effectiveness.

AILF 1: AI for Ethical and Responsible Leadership FELS 1, Professional and Ethical Norms

Leadership focus: Leaders shall ensure that all AI use is ethical, responsible, and aligned with professional norms to promote the academic success and well-being of all students.

Safeguards: protect student and staff data privacy in all AI applications; identify and mitigate potential bias in AI tools and outputs; establish clear guidelines for responsible AI use; promote equitable access to AI resources and opportunities.

AILF 2: AI for Vision, Strategy, and Change Management FELS 2, Vision and Mission

Leadership focus: Leaders shall establish, communicate, and sustain a shared vision for the strategic and ethical use of AI, aligned with the school and district mission, instructional priorities, and goals for student success.

Safeguards: support development and communication of AI expectations aligned to the School Improvement Plan and district strategic priorities; involve staff, students, and stakeholders; enhance teaching, learning, and student outcomes; monitor progress toward defined AI integration goals.

AILF 3: AI for Organizational Management FELS 3, School Operations, Management, and Safety

Leadership focus: Leaders shall leverage AI to strengthen organizational systems, increase operational efficiency, and support strategic decision-making aligned to a safe school environment, supervision, emergency procedures, and student and staff well-being.

Safeguards: improve scheduling, communication, and resource allocation; integrate AI insights into school improvement planning and data review; streamline operational tasks to maximize time for instructional leadership; identify inefficiencies and data-informed improvements.

AILF 4: AI for Student Learning and Continuous School Improvement FELS 4

Leadership focus: Leaders shall strategically utilize AI to enhance data-informed decision-making, strengthen intervention systems, and improve measurable student outcomes for all student groups.

Safeguards: systematically analyze student performance, subgroup, and longitudinal data; inform intervention design and progress monitoring; conduct data analysis aligned to School Improvement Plan goals; support equitable access to rigorous learning for all students.

AILF 5: AI for Inclusive and Supportive Learning Environments FELS 5, Learning Environment

Leadership focus: Leaders shall ensure that AI is used to cultivate a caring, inclusive, rigorous, and supportive learning environment that promotes student belonging, engagement, well-being, safety, and academic success for all students.

Safeguards: promote a positive, inclusive, student-centered culture; reinforce high expectations for behavior and engagement; monitor environments for rigor, safety, and respect; support staff in addressing social, emotional, behavioral, and academic needs.

AILF 6: AI for Capacity Building and Talent Development FELS 6, Recruitment and Professional Learning

Leadership focus: Leaders shall ensure that AI is strategically used to strengthen recruitment, professional learning, instructional coaching, and staff support systems that build the capacity of instructional personnel to improve teaching, learning, and student outcomes.

Safeguards: differentiate professional learning based on teacher needs and experience; design job-embedded learning aligned to school goals; strengthen instructional coaching cycles; support recruitment, onboarding, and retention systems.

AILF 7: AI for Leadership and Innovation FELS 7, Building Leadership Expertise

Leadership focus: Leaders shall ensure that AI is strategically used to strengthen leadership expertise, distributed leadership structures, succession planning, and organizational leadership capacity to support long-term school improvement and sustainability.

Safeguards: strengthen distributed leadership and clarify responsibilities; support assistant principal, coach, and teacher-leader growth; support succession planning and leadership continuity; analyze leadership challenges through multiple lenses.

AILF 8: AI for Communication and Partnerships FELS 8, Meaningful Parent, Family and Community Engagement

Leadership focus: Leaders shall ensure that AI is used to strengthen reciprocal communication, build trusting relationships, and promote meaningful collaboration with families and the community to support student success and well-being.

Safeguards: communicate clearly, consistently, and respectfully with families; differentiate communication to meet diverse family needs; analyze stakeholder feedback to inform decisions; establish systems for two-way communication and ongoing collaboration.

Human leadership, always

Across all eight domains, the AILF is explicit that AI tools, including Microsoft Copilot, are meant to enhance, not replace, human leadership, professional expertise, ethical judgment, and student-centered decision-making. Final decisions remain human.

The full AILF document, including detailed classroom and leadership examples for each domain (walkthrough tools, PLC agendas, coaching notes, family communication, and more), is available from BCPS as a downloadable reference for administrators.

Parent Resources

How student data is protected

The protections behind every AI decision. Read the plain-language answer first, then open the full document. This section will also host BCPS's Digital Learning Blueprint (Ethical Implementation Guide) once BCPS finalizes the disclosure language for its current draft status.

Sample answers for layout. Final wording to be confirmed by BCPS legal and data governance, including any Florida-specific privacy law.
What information is collected, and why?
Approved tools collect only what is needed to deliver the educational service. Information is used for that purpose alone, not for advertising or any unrelated commercial use.
Where is data stored, and who has access?
Data stays within the district's protected systems and approved vendor environments governed by data privacy agreements. Access is limited by role to the staff who need it for instruction and support.
Can AI train on my child's information?
No. Approved tools are configured so student and staff information is not used to train public AI models. This is a condition of approval for every tool listed on this site.
What are my rights as a parent?
Under FERPA you may inspect and review your child's education records, request corrections, and consent before personally identifiable information is shared outside permitted uses. Under PPRA, certain surveys require notice and the chance to opt out.
How are tools checked before students use them?
Every tool clears privacy, security, and accessibility review. Vendors sign data privacy agreements, must meet COPPA requirements for younger students, and may not sell student data or use it for targeted advertising.
Are AI detectors used to accuse students?
No. AI detection tools can be unreliable and biased, so they are not relied upon to make decisions about a student. Academic integrity is handled through clear expectations and honest conversation.

The laws that protect your child

FERPA

Federal law giving parents rights over education records and control of personal information.

COPPA

Federal rule protecting the online data of children under 13. Vendors must comply.

PPRA

Requires notice and opt-out for certain surveys on sensitive topics.

State privacy law

Limits how education vendors use student data, including a ban on selling it.

Your choices

Questions and opt-out

Families can ask how AI is used in their child's classroom and learn about available choices. A clear opt-out and contact process will be listed here for BCPS to complete.

Report a concern

A direct channel for families to raise a question or concern about an AI tool, routed to the responsible office. Contact details to be added by BCPS.

AI literacy

Understanding AI, for students and families

Broward teaches students not just to use AI, but to understand it, question it, and use it responsibly. The approach is built on the AI4K12 Five Big Ideas, the leading K-12 AI literacy framework.

The Five Big Ideas in AI

Perception

AI senses the world through cameras and microphones and turns signals into meaning.

Representation & Reasoning

AI builds models of the world to work through problems, but does not think like a person.

Learning

Computers learn by finding patterns in large amounts of data supplied by people.

Natural Interaction

Interacting naturally with people takes many kinds of knowledge that AI still lacks.

Societal Impact

AI can help and harm. Biased data can leave some people worse served, so ethics matter.

What students learn, by grade band

Elementary

  • Recognize where AI shows up in everyday life and that it is a human-made tool.
  • Understand computers learn differently from people, by finding patterns in data.
  • Begin reasoning about fairness, and that altered photos and videos exist.

Middle

  • Train and test a simple model and examine its data for bias.
  • Understand how recommendation algorithms shape what they see online.
  • Define AI and generative AI and weigh benefits and drawbacks.

High

  • Carry out a full machine-learning process and evaluate the data critically.
  • Judge AI against fairness, transparency, accountability, and privacy.
  • Evaluate AI-generated content for accuracy, bias, origin, and citation ethics.

How AI supports learning by content area

Leading with the strongest evidence. AI is a force multiplier for practice, feedback, and access. It does not replace teachers, and it does not match one-to-one human tutoring.

Special Education (ESE)

Text-to-speech and other supports remove barriers, aligned with Universal Design for Learning. This is the strongest-evidence area.

Mathematics

Adaptive practice gives students targeted problems and feedback, with the teacher guiding instruction.

Literacy

AI reading tutors give real-time feedback that multiplies guided oral-reading practice.

English Learners (ESOL)

Speech-feedback tools support private pronunciation practice. Translation helps, with accuracy limits noted.

Science

Automated formative feedback on how students investigate, supporting inquiry.

All subjects

Teachers save time on planning and differentiation, with every output reviewed by a human.

Learning · Department of Innovative Learning

AI Liaisons and AI Student Leaders

A structured, districtwide leadership program, not just a directory. AI Liaisons and AI Student Leaders work together at each school to build responsible AI use, student voice, and digital citizenship.

A message from the Department of Innovative Learning

"This program was created to empower students to explore artificial intelligence through a leadership lens, one that emphasizes ethics, collaboration, curiosity, and responsibility. AI Student Leaders are not expected to be experts. Instead, they are learners, ambassadors of positive digital citizenship, and partners in helping schools and the district think intentionally about the future of learning."

Dr. Sherri N. Wilson, Director, Innovative Learning

Program structure and timeline

School-based engagement

Roughly one meeting per month with a school-based facilitator, during or after school. Schools may fold this into leadership, media, innovation, or advisory structures.

District-level engagement

Students may be invited periodically, not routinely, to student-voice conversations, innovation showcases, and feedback sessions tied to district initiatives.

Time commitment

Designed to be manageable and inclusive. No weekly requirement. Students typically serve one school year, with flexibility to rotate.

Student selection guidance

Selection is about curiosity and digital citizenship, not credentials.

What selection looks for

  • Curiosity and willingness to engage with new ideas
  • Responsible digital citizenship and respectful communication
  • Reflects the diversity of the school community

What selection does not require

  • GPA or standardized test scores
  • Prior AI or technology experience
  • An existing formal leadership title

For staff and leaders

AI guidance for district and school leadership

Artificial intelligence for a district or school leader is best framed not just as a classroom tool, but as a system-level capability that affects instruction, operations, equity, and community trust. This guidance is written for clerical staff, specialists and facilitators, and leaders, not for families. See Parent Resources and Our Principles for the family-facing versions of this guidance.

Ethical AI use: remember the 3 principles

Fairness: treat all voices and perspectives with respect. Accuracy: always fact-check AI-generated information. Transparency: be clear when and how AI is used.

Role-based guidance

For clerical staff

DO

  • Draft emails, summarize meetings, suggest next steps
  • Organize and classify documents
  • Provide quick research or background information

DO NOT

  • Send communications without human review
  • Make decisions about personnel, students, or policy
  • Handle or store confidential data outside authorized systems

For specialists and facilitators

DO

  • Improve training design and professional learning materials
  • Analyze data for continuous improvement
  • Support project management, scheduling, and coordination

DO NOT

  • Let AI make instructional or professional learning decisions alone
  • Input confidential student, staff, or district data into unapproved tools
  • Select instructional resources or partnerships without human evaluation

For leaders

DO

  • Synthesize data, forecast scenarios, generate strategic briefs
  • Draft memos, meeting summaries, and community updates
  • Keep the human in the loop and always review output

DO NOT

  • Allow AI to make personnel, staffing, or evaluative decisions
  • Rely on AI to interpret Board policies, contracts, or statutes
  • Use AI as the source of truth for improvement or technology decisions
